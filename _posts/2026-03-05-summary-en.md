## [First Direct Observation of Atomic 'Mouse Bite' Defects in Chips Could Revolutionize Semiconductor R&D](https://news.mydrivers.com/1/1106/1106922.htm) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A research team led by Cornell University, in collaboration with TSMC and ASM, has for the first time directly observed atomic-scale 'mouse bite' defects at chip interfaces using a novel high-resolution 3D electron microscopy technique. Their findings, published in Nature Communications on February 23, 2026, reveal subtle roughness and defects formed during the optimized growth process of transistors. This breakthrough matters because directly visualizing atomic-scale defects provides an unprecedented tool for debugging and failure analysis during chip development, especially as transistors shrink to nanoscale dimensions. It has the potential to improve the reliability and performance of nearly all modern electronics, from smartphones and AI data centers to quantum computers, by enabling manufacturers to better understand and control the manufacturing process. The imaging method is based on the Electron Microscope Pixel Array Detector (EMPAD) technology, which allowed the team to track the positions of individual atoms and quantify interface roughness in prototype gate-all-around transistors. The research specifically focused on defects at the interface of the 3D gate oxide and the silicon channel, which are critical for transistor performance.

## [US research team proposes using gravitational wave background to measure Hubble constant and resolve Hubble tension](https://www.space.com/astronomy/how-fast-is-the-universe-actually-expanding-ripples-in-spacetime-could-finally-solve-hubble-tension) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A research team from the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign and the University of Chicago has proposed a new technique called the 'stochastic siren method' to measure the Hubble constant using the stochastic gravitational wave background (GWB) from distant black hole mergers. The team estimates that with improving detector sensitivity, this method could provide an independent measurement of the Hubble constant within the next six years. This matters because it offers a novel, independent pathway to resolve the long-standing 'Hubble tension' problem in cosmology, where different measurement methods yield conflicting values for the universe's expansion rate. A resolution could either validate our current cosmological model or force a major revision, impacting our fundamental understanding of the universe's composition and evolution. The method works by analyzing the strength of the stochastic gravitational wave background signal, which depends on the volume of space and the density of black hole mergers, both of which are influenced by the Hubble constant. The principle has been validated using existing detectors like LIGO, but practical application awaits more sensitive future observations.

telegram · zaihuapd · Mar 4, 16:54

**Background**: The Hubble constant (H0) quantifies the current expansion rate of the universe, describing how fast galaxies recede from us, with speed proportional to their distance. The 'Hubble tension' refers to the statistically significant discrepancy between the value of H0 measured from the early universe (e.g., via the cosmic microwave background) and that measured from the local, late universe (e.g., via supernovae). The stochastic gravitational wave background is a persistent, random signal composed of countless unresolved gravitational waves from sources like merging black holes throughout the cosmos.
