## [Researchers reverse-engineer Apple M4 Neural Engine, revealing 38 TOPS claim as misleading](https://maderix.substack.com/p/inside-the-m4-apple-neural-engine) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Researchers, including maderix, successfully reverse-engineered Apple's M4 Neural Engine (ANE) by bypassing the CoreML framework and directly accessing its private _ANEClient API. Their benchmarks revealed the ANE's actual peak FP16 performance is 19 TFLOPS, not the advertised 38 TOPS, and found no hardware acceleration for INT8 operations. This independent verification exposes a significant discrepancy between Apple's marketing claims and the hardware's real-world performance, which is crucial for developers and researchers benchmarking AI workloads. It highlights the pitfalls of relying solely on vendor-provided TOPS figures and underscores the importance of independent, low-level hardware analysis for accurate performance evaluation. The study found the ANE's on-chip SRAM is about 32MB, its convolution operations are 3x faster than matrix multiplication, and bypassing CoreML can improve throughput for small operations by 2-4x. The chip's peak power efficiency was measured at approximately 6.6 TFLOPS/W, which is about 80 times that of an Nvidia A100 GPU.

telegram · zaihuapd · Mar 2, 08:00

**Background**: The Apple Neural Engine (ANE) is a series of dedicated AI accelerators integrated into Apple's A-series and M-series SoCs, first introduced in 2017. TOPS (Trillions of Operations Per Second) and TFLOPS (Tera Floating Point Operations Per Second) are key metrics for comparing AI hardware, but they measure different things: TOPS often counts integer operations, while TFLOPS measures floating-point calculations. In marketing, it's common for vendors to multiply FP16 performance by 2 to derive a TOPS figure, as INT8 operations can theoretically be executed twice as fast on specialized hardware.

## [ECH Protocol Completes Final IETF Approval, RFC 9849 Nears Publication](https://www.rfc-editor.org/auth48/rfc9849) ⭐️ 8.0/10

The Encrypted Client Hello (ECH) protocol has completed the final AUTH48 approval stage by all authors, IANA, and area directors in late February 2026, receiving the assigned RFC number 9849. The protocol is now pending the resolution of one final GitHub technical issue (#1308) before its official publication. This final approval marks the culmination of a seven-year effort to close a major privacy gap in TLS 1.3 by encrypting previously exposed handshake metadata. Widespread adoption by major browsers and platforms like Chrome, Firefox, Android, and Cloudflare signifies a fundamental shift towards stronger default internet privacy, making it harder for network observers to identify visited websites. ECH specifically encrypts the Server Name Indication (SNI) and Application-Layer Protocol Negotiation (ALPN) metadata within the TLS ClientHello message, which were previously sent in plaintext. The protocol is only compatible with TLS 1.3, as it relies on cryptographic mechanisms introduced in that version.

telegram · zaihuapd · Mar 2, 10:28

**Background**: Transport Layer Security (TLS) is the protocol that encrypts communication between web browsers and servers, indicated by the 'https://' in URLs. In the TLS handshake, the ClientHello message traditionally sent the Server Name Indication (SNI) in plaintext to tell the server which website the client wants to connect to, especially when multiple sites are hosted on one IP address. This SNI leak became a significant privacy concern as it allowed network observers, including ISPs and censors, to see which websites a user is visiting, even if the actual content of the visit was encrypted by TLS 1.3.

The rule is set to take effect on January 15, 2026.
