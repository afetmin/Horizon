## [Research Shows LLMs Suffer Major Performance Drop in Multi-Turn Conversations, GPT-5 Loses 33% Accuracy](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.06120) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A new study published on arXiv (2505.06120) finds that large language models (LLMs), including cutting-edge models like GPT-5, suffer a severe performance drop in multi-turn conversations, with an average accuracy loss of 39% and a loss of up to 33% for the most advanced models. The research, analyzing over 200,000 simulated conversations, identifies that models often make incorrect assumptions early in a dialogue and struggle to self-correct, leading them to 'get lost' in complex interactions. This finding is critical because multi-turn conversations are fundamental to real-world AI applications like customer service chatbots, AI assistants, and collaborative problem-solving tools. The significant performance degradation reveals a major bottleneck for the practical reliability and deployment of state-of-the-art LLMs, challenging the assumption that more capable models inherently handle extended interactions well. The performance drop persists even for specific tasks like Python coding and is not effectively mitigated by common techniques like lowering the sampling temperature. The researchers suggest that when a conversation goes off track, users should reset the model's state by summarizing previous requirements and starting a new conversation.

**Background**: Multi-turn dialogue evaluation is a key area in AI research, with benchmarks like ConvBench and MT-Bench++ designed to test specific abilities such as topic shifting and instruction retention across multiple conversation turns. Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-5 are trained on vast datasets to generate human-like text, but their performance in maintaining coherence and accuracy over a series of interconnected exchanges (multi-turn conversations) is a distinct and challenging capability. Performance degradation in long contexts is a known issue, but this research systematically quantifies the problem specifically for conversational tasks.
